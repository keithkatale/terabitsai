# Professional Writing & Copywriting Skill

Universal writing skill for AI agents. Applies to cold email, sales copy, content writing, business communication, reports, and any professional text output.

---

## 1. Core Writing Principles

Every piece of writing must satisfy these nine properties:

| Principle | Meaning | Test |
|---|---|---|
| **Clarity** | Understood on first read, no ambiguity | Could a stranger parse the meaning without context? |
| **Conciseness** | No wasted words; every sentence earns its place | Can you cut 20% without losing meaning? Do it. |
| **Correctness** | Grammar, facts, names, numbers — all verified | Read aloud. Does anything sound off? |
| **Completeness** | Reader has everything they need to act | Would the reader need to reply "what do you mean?" |
| **Coherence** | Ideas flow logically; each sentence connects to the next | Cover the intro → remove it. Does the body still make sense? |
| **Consideration** | Written for the reader, not the writer | Does it answer "what's in it for me?" from the reader's perspective? |
| **Courtesy** | Respectful and professional regardless of context | Would you send this to your most important client? |
| **Concreteness** | Specific facts, numbers, examples — not vague claims | Replace every "many" with a number, every "soon" with a date. |
| **Consistency** | Tone, terminology, and formatting match prior communications | Does this read like it came from the same person/brand? |

## 2. Tone & Register Adaptation

Match tone to context. Never default to one style.

### Formal
- No contractions ("do not" not "don't")
- Third person ("the team" not "we")
- Precise vocabulary, no slang or idioms
- **Use for:** Legal, compliance, executive reports, first contact with unknown stakeholders, press releases

### Standard / Professional
- Light contractions OK ("we'll", "it's")
- Second person ("you") for engagement
- Clear and direct, but warm
- **Use for:** Client emails, proposals, documentation, internal reports, stakeholder updates

### Conversational
- Contractions, shorter sentences, casual vocabulary
- First person plural ("we") or second person ("you")
- Personality shows through
- **Use for:** Marketing content, social media, blog posts, cold outreach, team Slack, onboarding copy

### Rule of Thumb
- When in doubt, match the register of the most recent message from the recipient.
- Never be more casual than the person you're writing to.
- Never be more formal than the context demands — it reads as cold or robotic.

## 3. Copywriting Frameworks

Use these frameworks as structural templates. Pick the one that fits the goal.

### AIDA — Attention, Interest, Desire, Action
**Best for:** Product launches, feature announcements, landing pages, webinar invites

```
Attention: Open with a bold stat, question, or contrarian statement
Interest:  Explain the problem or opportunity — make them lean in
Desire:    Show transformation: benefits, social proof, outcomes
Action:    One clear CTA — what should they do RIGHT NOW?
```

**Example (product email):**
> Subject: Your checkout loses 68% of buyers before payment
>
> Most Shopify stores bleed revenue at checkout — slow load times,
> no guest option, confusing layouts.
>
> We rebuilt checkout flows for 200+ stores. Average result: 34% more
> completed purchases in 30 days.
>
> Want to see what your store's checkout could look like?
> → [Book a free audit](link)

---

### PAS — Problem, Agitate, Solution
**Best for:** Cold outreach, re-engagement, sales sequences, objection handling

```
Problem:  Name their specific pain (be precise, not generic)
Agitate:  Intensify — show what happens if they don't fix it
Solution: Present your offer as the relief
```

**Example (cold email):**
> Subject: Your mobile conversion rate
>
> Noticed your store loads in 6+ seconds on mobile.
> That's costing you roughly 40% of visitors who bounce before
> the page finishes — every day, silently.
>
> We optimized mobile load times for [similar company] and their
> conversion rate jumped 28% in the first month.
>
> Open to a 15-minute look at what's happening?

---

### BAB — Before, After, Bridge
**Best for:** Case studies, transformation narratives, testimonials, story-driven content

```
Before: Paint the current painful reality (specific, relatable)
After:  Show the desired future state (concrete outcomes)
Bridge: Your product/service/idea is how they get from Before to After
```

---

### PASTOR — Problem, Amplify, Story, Transformation, Offer, Response
**Best for:** Long-form sales pages, detailed proposals, fundraising

```
Problem:        Identify and name it
Amplify:        Show consequences of inaction
Story:          Tell a relatable narrative (client case, personal experience)
Transformation: Show the measurable change
Offer:          Present what you're offering and why it works
Response:       Ask for the specific next step
```

---

### Hook → Story → CTA
**Best for:** Social media posts, LinkedIn content, short-form marketing

```
Hook:  3 seconds to stop the scroll. Use a surprising stat, bold claim,
       or question that creates an information gap.
Story: Deliver value — insight, lesson, data, personal experience.
       Use BAB or PAS structure within the story.
CTA:   One clear ask. Comment, click, reply, share.
```

---

### Observation + Question
**Best for:** Cold email (highest-performing formula at 4.2% reply rate)

```
One specific observation about THEIR situation (proves you did homework)
+ One genuine question (opens conversation, doesn't sell)
```

**Example:**
> Subject: Your Austin store
>
> Saw you just expanded to a second location on South Congress — congrats.
>
> Curious: are you seeing the same traffic patterns online as in-store,
> or is the web lagging behind the physical growth?

## 4. Email Writing Rules

### Subject Lines (Spend 50% of your time here)
- **Length:** 2-7 words. Mobile truncates at 33-43 characters — front-load key info.
- **Personalization:** Include their name, company, or a specific detail. Personalized subjects hit 46% open rate vs. 35% without.
- **Format:** Lowercase or sentence case. No ALL CAPS. No exclamation marks.
- **Questions work:** Question-format subjects achieve 46% open rate.
- **Avoid:** Numbers in subject lines (slightly hurt performance), clickbait, generic phrases ("Quick question", "Touching base").

### Body
- **Length:** 50-75 words for cold email (12% response rate). Every word beyond 75 lowers reply rate.
- **Opening line:** State the reason you're writing or a specific observation. Never start with "I hope this email finds you well" or "My name is..."
- **Paragraphs:** Maximum 3 sentences each. Single-sentence paragraphs are fine.
- **Plain text:** No HTML templates for B2B outreach. Plain text looks human-written and outperforms designed emails.
- **One idea per email.** If you need to cover multiple topics, send multiple emails or use clear sections.

### Call to Action
- **One CTA per email.** Multiple CTAs split attention and reduce response.
- **Low-friction:** "Open to a quick look?" beats "Schedule a 60-minute demo."
- **Question format:** End with a question — it invites a reply, not a decision.
- **Never ask for too much in email #1.** First email starts a conversation. Selling comes later.

### Follow-ups
- **Spacing:** 3-5 days between emails. Never 1-day follow-up cadence.
- **Add new value** in each follow-up — don't just "bump" or "check in."
- **Sequence length:** 3-4 emails maximum. After that, you're spam.
- **Timing:** Thursday 9-11 AM achieves 44% open rate (best slot).

### Signature
- Name | Title | Company | URL
- No logos, no social links, no inspirational quotes. Clean and professional.

## 5. Persuasion Techniques

### Power Words (use sparingly, strategically)

| Category | Words | Use Case |
|---|---|---|
| **Urgency** | Now, Today, Limited, Expiring, Deadline, Instant | CTAs, time-sensitive offers |
| **Exclusivity** | Exclusive, Invitation, Members-only, Private, VIP | Premium positioning |
| **Trust** | Proven, Guaranteed, Reliable, Verified, Backed | Overcoming objections |
| **Curiosity** | Secret, Revealed, Surprising, Hidden, Untapped | Hooks, subject lines |
| **Value** | Free, Bonus, Effortless, Simple, Unlock | Reducing friction |
| **Results** | Boost, Transform, Accelerate, Double, Skyrocket | Benefit statements |
| **Safety** | Protected, Secure, Risk-free, Certified, Tested | Trust-building |

### Psychological Principles

1. **Social Proof** — "200+ stores use this" beats "our product is great." Numbers > adjectives.
2. **Specificity** — "34% increase in 30 days" beats "significant improvement." Precise numbers feel more credible.
3. **Loss Aversion** — People fear losing more than they desire gaining. "You're losing $X/month" > "You could save $X/month."
4. **Reciprocity** — Give value before asking. Lead with an insight, audit, or free resource.
5. **Scarcity** — Limited availability increases perceived value. But only use if real — fake scarcity destroys trust.
6. **Authority** — Cite data sources, expert opinions, or recognized brands. "According to [source]" > "Studies show."
7. **Contrast** — Show the gap between current state and desired state. The bigger the contrast, the stronger the motivation.

## 6. Business Communication Templates

### Internal Status Update
```
Subject: [Project] — Week of [date] update

Progress:
- [Completed item 1]
- [Completed item 2]

Blockers:
- [Blocker, if any — include what's needed to unblock]

Next week:
- [Priority 1]
- [Priority 2]

[No sign-off needed for internal updates]
```

### Client Proposal Email
```
Subject: [Specific deliverable] for [Company]

Hi [Name],

Following our conversation about [topic], here's what we propose:

[2-3 sentence summary of the approach and expected outcome]

Deliverables:
- [Item 1]
- [Item 2]
- [Item 3]

Timeline: [X weeks]
Investment: [Amount or range]

I've attached the full proposal. Happy to walk through it on a quick call
this week — would [Day] or [Day] work?

[Signature]
```

### Decline / Bad News
```
Subject: Update on [topic]

Hi [Name],

Thank you for [their effort/proposal/request].

After reviewing [specific factor], we've decided to [decision].
[One sentence of honest reasoning — no corporate fluff.]

[If applicable: alternative, next steps, or what would change the outcome]

Appreciate your time on this.

[Signature]
```

## 7. Content Writing Guidelines

### Headlines
- **Specificity wins:** "How We Increased Checkout Revenue 34% in 30 Days" > "How to Improve Your Store"
- **Numbers perform:** Odd numbers outperform even numbers in headlines (research-backed).
- **Promise a benefit or answer a question.** If the headline doesn't make them want to read sentence #1, rewrite it.

### Structure for Long-Form
1. **Hook** (1-2 sentences): Why should they keep reading?
2. **Context** (1 paragraph): What's the problem or opportunity?
3. **Body** (main content): Deliver on the headline's promise. Use subheadings every 200-300 words.
4. **Proof** (examples, data, case studies): Back claims with evidence.
5. **CTA** (1-2 sentences): What should they do next?

### Readability
- **Sentence length:** Mix short (5-10 words) with medium (15-20). Avoid 30+ word sentences.
- **Paragraph length:** 1-4 sentences. White space is your friend.
- **Reading level:** Aim for 8th grade (Flesch-Kincaid). Simpler language = wider audience.
- **Active voice:** "We increased revenue by 34%" not "Revenue was increased by 34%."
- **Cut filler:** Delete "very," "really," "just," "actually," "basically," "in order to," "it is important to note that."

## 8. Writing Anti-Patterns (Never Do These)

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Starting with "I" | Self-centered; reader doesn't care about you first | Start with their problem, situation, or a relevant observation |
| "I hope this finds you well" | Generic filler; screams mass email | Delete it. Start with substance. |
| Exclamation marks in professional writing | Reads as overexcited or insincere | Period. Let the words carry the energy. |
| Em dashes in cold email | Looks AI-generated or over-polished | Use commas, periods, or parentheses |
| Vague claims ("industry-leading") | Meaningless without proof | Replace with specific numbers or outcomes |
| Wall of text | Nobody reads a 300-word paragraph | Break into 1-3 sentence paragraphs |
| Multiple CTAs | Decision paralysis; lower response | One CTA per communication |
| Smart quotes / fancy formatting | Formatting breaks across email clients | Plain text, straight quotes |
| Placeholder variables left in | Destroys credibility instantly | Triple-check all `{{variables}}` before sending |
| "Let me know if you have any questions" | Passive; puts burden on reader | Ask a specific question or propose a next step |

## 9. Editing Checklist

Run every piece of writing through this checklist before finalizing:

```
□ Read it aloud. Does it sound natural?
□ Cut 20% of the words. (You can almost always cut 20%.)
□ Is the CTA clear and singular?
□ Does every paragraph advance the core message?
□ Are all names, numbers, dates, and links correct?
□ Is the tone appropriate for the audience and context?
□ Would the first sentence make someone keep reading?
□ Is there a specific benefit or insight within the first 2 lines?
□ Are paragraphs ≤ 4 sentences?
□ Did you use active voice? (Ctrl+F "was", "were", "been" — rewrite passives)
□ Did you eliminate filler words? ("very", "really", "just", "actually", "basically")
□ For email: is it under 75 words (cold) or under 200 words (warm)?
□ For email: does the subject line work on mobile (< 40 chars)?
```

## 10. Framework Selection Guide

| Situation | Best Framework | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Cold email to a stranger | **Observation + Question** or **PAS** | Highest reply rates; shows research; low-pressure |
| Follow-up email | **BAB** (mini version) | Paints quick before/after, adds new value |
| Product launch announcement | **AIDA** | Builds progressive engagement to CTA |
| Sales page / long proposal | **PASTOR** | Comprehensive persuasion with story + proof |
| Social media post | **Hook → Story → CTA** | Optimized for scroll-stopping + engagement |
| Case study / testimonial | **BAB** | Natural transformation narrative |
| Objection handling reply | **PAS** (compressed) | Acknowledges concern, reframes, provides solution |
| Internal report / update | **Direct structure** (no framework needed) | Lead with conclusion, then supporting details |
| Bad news delivery | **Buffer → Reason → Decision → Alternative** | Respectful, honest, forward-looking |
| Re-engagement (lapsed contact) | **PAS** or **Observation + Question** | Reignites relevance; shows you noticed their silence |
